Experiencing paranoia when testing the Oxford vaccine



Jack Sommers had a test shot of the Covid-19 vaccine, studied by AstraZeneca and Oxford University, with the only side effect being paranoia. Share on Independent, Sommers said he was one of the earliest registered to test the vaccine. Here is his vaccination experience. Jack Sommers, Oxford Volunteer Vaccination Volunteer at St George's Hospital. Photo: Independent. Jack Sommers, Oxford Volunteer Vaccination Volunteer at St George's Hospital. Photo: Independent. I was paid a total of 235 pounds (about 7.3 million) for 5 visits to the hospital, getting a lot of needles in my arm, doing a "guinea pig" experiment, hoping to help develop the Covid vaccine- 19. First, you must provide a urine and blood sample, fill out the form, and watch a video of the manufacturer only expecting you to have mild side effects. Then you go home waiting for the results. Two days after my first visit at the hospital, I kept sending emails asking why I had not been called for a vaccination. The answer is: "Calm down and wait". That was the first time I felt nervous.


My worries quickly evaporated when a nurse talked about vaccinations that had been given to rhesus monkeys. I bowed to the sacrifices of the monkeys for medicine and the image of the "guinea pig" in my head disappeared. The only injection I received was painless. I was given a thermometer by the coordinator and asked to record my symptoms daily through an online diary. The biggest side effect I had was paranoia. I don't know if I was in the 50% group that got the vaccine or the 50% got the placebo, but twice during the first week, I had mild fever, or it was my imagination. "The temperature rose and sneezed," I wrote in my diary on Tuesday. However, my temperature that day was 36.5 - completely normal, so my story doesn't always match reality. As the weeks passed, I did not experience any additional symptoms. Instead, my preoccupation is experimentation. I didn't care if Imperial or Oxford had vaccines first, but every good news about the Oxford vaccine made me happy. Later, I was very sad to learn that the monkeys that were vaccinated were infected with COV.




Failure and learning are the core of scientists' success, so even if the vaccine doesn't work, I'm comfortable being the first to put my name on the trial list. I thought of a message from a woman with multiple sclerosis: "You are helping millions of people." This made me - a grown man who disliked needles and covered his face while watching horror movies, bravely stepped into a one-of-a-kind experiment in history. On November 23, AstraZeneca reported that the Covid-19 vaccine was 70 percent effective in critical trials and up to 90 percent effective depending on the dose given, and there were no serious side effects. The vaccine can be transported and stored at normal refrigerator temperatures, making it easier to distribute in poor countries.
